In most studies of the Maxwell Equations, the [[Manuscript/Knowledge/Science/Physics/Electromagnetism/The Maxwell Equations#Definition|regular forms]] of the equations are used since they are the most appropriate for electromagnetism **in the vacuum**. However, in the study of RF systems, we almost never consider wave behavior in the vacuum, unless we are considering the behavior of waves in air (which we approximate to the vacuum). More often we consider the behavior of waves **within some dielectric material or waveguide** where the usual wave equations become burdensome.

## The Reasoning
You may rightly ask why these equations are more friendly than their vacuum forms. Inside a dielectric material, there are two types of charge and two types of currents:
- Free charges and free currents, and
- Bound charges and bound currents.
Free charges & currents are those which we control, namely, the conducting electrons, external currents, and charges placed upon materials. Bound charges & currents are those which are trapped within atoms, yet which still move, namely the electrons in and below the [[Valence Band|valence band]] and the currents caused by their movements.

Since we only care about free charges & currents, it is useful to make the bound charges & currents **implicit** in our equations so we can focus on what we care about. All charge movements and transient currents due to **dipoles** are therefore put out of our sight.
