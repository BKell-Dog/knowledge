Electric charge can be positive or negative with various magnitudes, and a charged particle exerts a force on another charged particle depending on the charge of each. The force between charges has the following properties:
1. Charges of the same polarity repel, those of different polarity attract.
2. The magnitude of the force is proportional to the product of the charge magnitudes, $q_1$ and $q_2$.
3. The magnitude of the force is inversely proportional to the square of the distance between the two charges, $r^2$.
4. The magnitude of the force is dependent on the medium in which the two charges are found. If we represent this medium using a property of 'permittivity', $\epsilon$, then the magnitude in inversely proportional to this. In a vacuum the medium is the empty [electromagnetic field](https://grokipedia.com/page/Electromagnetic_field) and the permittivity of the field is the [vacuum permittivity](https://grokipedia.com/page/Vacuum_permittivity), $\epsilon_0$.

Thus,
$$
F_{e} \propto \frac{q_{1} \> q_{2}}{\epsilon\>r^2}
$$
Experimentally it is known that this equation requires a constant of proportionality, $k$, to become equal. So,
$$
F_{e} = k\>\frac{q_{1} \> q_{2}}{\>r^2}
$$
Note that since $\epsilon$ is a constant, we draw that value out into $k$.

This equation represents the magnitude only; if we want to fully describe the electric force between charges, we must also account for direction by making this a vector quantity. We do this by adding a unit vector which sits at charge one and points directly away charge one towards charge two. So finally,
$$
\hat{F}_{e} = k\>(q_{1} \> q_{2})\> (\frac{\hat{r}_{12}}{|\vec{r}_{12}|^2})=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon}\frac{q_1 \> q_2 \> \hat{r}_{12}}{|\vec{r}_{12}|^2}
$$
where $|\vec{r}_{12}|$ is the distance between charge the two charges, $q_{1}$ and $q_{2}$, and $\hat{r}_{12}$ is that unit vector originating at $q_{1}$ and pointing at $q_{2}$. Note that the constant $k$ has been found experimentally for all media and is equal to $\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon}$, where the epsilon term is the product of the electrical permittivity of the vacuum, $\epsilon_{0}$, and the electrical permittivity of the present medium, $\epsilon_{r}$, giving $\epsilon=\epsilon_{0}\epsilon_{r}$.

If the charges are the same polarity, both positive or both negative, then the force on each charge will point away from the other. Likewise, if the charges are opposite in polarity, one positive and one negative, then the force vectors will point inwards towards each other charge. Thus we have represented the attraction and repulsion of like and unlike charges.
